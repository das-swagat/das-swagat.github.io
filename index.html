My Story

Most work in artificial intelligence begins in controlled settings. Mine did not.

I started in electrical engineering and energy systems, working with real infrastructure where things rarely behave the way theory suggests. Spending years teaching, building labs, and working hands on with these systems shaped how I think about problems today. If something does not hold up outside the lab, it is not finished.

That perspective now drives my work as a PhD student at Virginia Commonwealth University. My research sits at the intersection of artificial intelligence and cyber physical systems, covering self supervised learning, anomaly detection, and perception tasks such as image segmentation, object detection, and multimodal data across street view, aerial, and LiDAR in both 2D and 3D. The goal is simple. Build systems that remain reliable when the data is messy, incomplete, and constantly changing.

More recently, I have been working on agentic LLM based cybersecurity systems for smart manufacturing environments. The focus is on building systems that can reason, adapt, and interact with complex industrial processes rather than just analyze them.

I tend to work in a hands on, systems driven way. That means building pipelines, working with large imperfect datasets, and pushing ideas all the way toward something that can actually be deployed and trusted.

Before this, I spent several years as an Assistant Professor, along with serving as Assistant Director for Quality Assurance. During that time, I worked on improving academic processes, strengthening accreditation outcomes, and contributing to the university’s performance across national and international ranking frameworks.

Outside research, I enjoy a different kind of problem solving. Sports and sports analytics, traveling, trying new cuisines, and meeting people from different backgrounds are all part of how I stay curious. Those experiences often shape how I approach systems, decisions, and impact.
